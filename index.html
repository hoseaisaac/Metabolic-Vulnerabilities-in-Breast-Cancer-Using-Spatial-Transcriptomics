Abstract

Spatially Resolved Metabolic Vulnerability Discovery

Metabolic reprogramming is a hallmark of cancer, but the spatial heterogeneity of metabolic programmes within the tumour microenvironment remains poorly characterised. Spatially resolved and cross-patient conserved metabolic vulnerabilities are needed to inform targeted therapies.


We present a spatially aware deep-learning framework for identifying proliferation-linked metabolic vulnerabilities in breast cancer using 10x Genomics Visium spatial transcriptomics. Our framework combines phenotype-linked prediction of proliferation from pathway activity, label-independent vulnerability scoring using autoencoder perturbation, and mechanistic support from flux inference with scFEA.


In leave-one-patient-out validation, the classifier achieved robust performance (mean AUC = 0.8813 ¬± 0.0604) and generalised to the Visium HD sample (AUC = 0.8275), supporting cross-platform applicability. Flux analysis identified seven metabolic modules that were consistently associated with proliferation across all patients.


Nucleotide metabolism emerged as the most reproducible vulnerability, with purine salvage and biosynthesis (M148), pyrimidine biosynthesis (M155), and nucleotide salvage (M159) ranking as the highest-confidence targets‚Äîall targetable with FDA-approved agents including methotrexate, gemcitabine, and 6-mercaptopurine.

Methods

Analysis Pipeline

A

Data Preprocessing

Quality control, normalisation, and dimensionality reduction of Visium data from seven breast cancer patients (24,422 spots).

B

Feature Construction

Pathway activity scores from 7,829 gene sets across five databases with FDR-corrected proliferation association testing.

C

Model Training

MetabolicVulnerabilityNet (64‚Üí32‚Üí16) with spatial block-wise cross-validation, benchmarked against logistic regression.

D

External Validation

Leave-one-patient-out cross-validation across all seven patients including Visium HD for cross-platform assessment.

E

Flux Modelling

scFEA flux inference, cross-patient module comparison, and therapeutic target prioritisation based on convergent evidence.

+

Autoencoder Analysis

Symmetric autoencoder (20‚Üí8‚Üí20) with in-silico pathway knockdown for label-independent vulnerability scoring.
